Ethnographic Case Study

User Research


A large part of this study involved recording videos of the person we interviewed, and analysing his thought process when using different search tools for design inspiration. The video and presentation can be made available on request!


This is a report of an ethnographic case study which was aimed at observing how designers working in large teams use google search to gain design inspiration and make design decisions while working in teams.

Me and a group of 2 other people interviewed a designer at Digital Telepathy. We interviewed the designer John (name withheld) about his daily design process.

Firstly, we made some key observations on how the workspace would affect the design process of the people involed. The office layout facilitated close interaction amongst designers and developers. There was no distinction in the role of the people in areas. The lunch area doubled as a meeting area for large team meetings and announcements. The following is a rough layout of the office.


We also gathered some information and drafted a rough persona of the person we were interviewing.
  • Senior UI/UX Designer
  • >5 years experience
  • Very organized person. Uses a Macbook Pro 15" and an external monitor for design work. We found a bunch of notebooks lying around and some diaries. We assumed, that he puts them to use for drafting ideas and planning his work.

Being a senior designer, John mentioned that "most of his work is already done", because he has already been in the field for a very long time and knows what has worked for him in the past. In terms of Google search, he said that he hated results that head already seen previously.

"I’m looking for the best product design practices, patterns, and something innovative and new."

How does John search for design information?

John mentioned that he relies on a more collaborative style of working and thus references his co-workers' projects and ideas most of the time. At other times, he looks at the work of competitors and performs competitive analysis in the design process. Some other tools he mentioned that he used for "passive search" include:
  • Product Design Weekly - He particularly liked this because it was a curated list of best product design tips and practices. He came to know about it from a co-worker. Since a lot of his co-workers are also subscribed to the same list, there was room for discussion on several common topics.
  • Dribble - To look for visual/aesthetic inspiration. He mainly uses it to stay up to date with the latest design trends but doesn’t really think it’s substantial.
  • Instagram - Mainly to look at aesthetic work but claims the lack of depth for it qualify as good design

There is a need for John to quickly reference a co-worker and/or competitor's work (that has been succesful in the past).

We observed after a brief talk with him, that he didn't really use Google search actively. On being asked to describe the search results he obtained through Dribbble:

What does John look for in a robust searching tool?
  • Search with Multiple Tags
  • Deep Dive browsing
  • Filtering Searches

Needs a way to search for case studies and workflows for design processes in the context of his work.

He usually searches for inspiration for individual elements of a website or composite design. and when looking through search results, spends a maximum of 1 second evaluating each design. The vast array of search results makes it harder for him to come to a decision.

Needs to receive short curated list of current design practices that he hasn’t already seen.

The above need adheres to the attributes of John's search habits - they are short, not repetitive and are of good quality since they have been curated. The following is solution we proposed taking John's input on the ideal design search tool".

"The ideal search tool would almost be like an intern."
  • No interface
  • Always online
  • Connected with mail, calendar and all communication channels
  • Able to understand John's search preferences, learn from his choices made online and return appropriate results, almost automating the design search process.
